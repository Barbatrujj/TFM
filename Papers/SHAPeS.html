Antoni Gaudí and the Architecture of Shape

Geometric Logic, Natural Morphogenesis, and Computational Formalization

Abstract

Antoni Gaudí (1852–1926) developed a formal system of shape generation that rejected classical Euclidean geometry in favor of continuous curvature, structural logic, and nature-derived morphologies. This document analyzes Gaudí’s primary shape systems, identifies their mathematical and physical foundations, and formalizes them for computational and AI-based shape generation.

1. Gaudí’s Philosophy of Shape

For Gaudí, shape is not imposed but discovered. Forms arise from gravity, material behavior, and growth logic. Geometry is inseparable from structure and function.

  • Shape follows force, not fashion
  • Nature is a generator, not a reference image
  • Continuity is preferred over segmentation
  • Structure and ornament are unified

2. Primary Geometric Families

2.1 Catenary Curves

The catenary curve is generated by a chain hanging under gravity. When inverted, it becomes an ideal compression arch. Gaudí used catenaries extensively as structural and formal generators.

  • Smooth, gravity-derived curvature
  • No pointed apex or constant radius
  • Strong sense of equilibrium

2.2 Parabolic and Hyperbolic Arches

Parabolic and hyperbolic curves appear in openings, vaults, and structural ribs, allowing efficient force distribution and expressive form.

3. Ruled Surface Geometry

Gaudí favored ruled surfaces because they can be constructed from straight lines while producing complex curvature.

3.1 Hyperbolic Paraboloids

  • Saddle-shaped, double-curved surfaces
  • Opposing curvature directions
  • High structural efficiency

3.2 Hyperboloids

  • Rotational ruled surfaces
  • Common in towers and spires
  • Enable light penetration and vertical emphasis

4. Helicoids and Spiral Forms

Helicoidal geometry expresses growth, ascent, and continuity. Gaudí employed spirals in staircases, columns, and decorative transitions.

  • Rotational motion along a vertical axis
  • No clear beginning or end
  • Strong association with natural growth patterns

5. Branching and Inclined Structures

Columns in Gaudí’s architecture behave like trees. They incline, bifurcate, and redistribute loads through branching systems.

  • Non-vertical structural axes
  • Progressive subdivision of supports
  • Cross-sections evolve with height

6. Surface Continuity and Edge Softening

Sharp edges are avoided in favor of blended transitions. Surfaces appear eroded, grown, or molded rather than assembled.

  • Rounded corners and filleted edges
  • Continuous surface flow
  • Tactile, bodily perception of form

7. Negative Space as Shape

Openings and voids are sculpted as positive forms. Windows, doors, and light wells follow the same geometric logic as solid elements.

8. Formalized Shape Logic for AI Systems

  • Begin with force-based curves
  • Generate surfaces through ruled geometry
  • Allow shapes to branch and transform
  • Maintain continuous curvature
  • Avoid rigid Euclidean primitives

9. Agent-Ready Pseudologic


Initialize Force System (gravity, compression)
Generate Catenary or Parabolic Curves
Construct Ruled Surfaces
Apply Branching Transformations
Soften Edges and Transitions
Output: Continuous Organic Shape System
