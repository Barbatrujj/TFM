Antoni Gaudí and the Architecture of Color

Chromatic Logic, Materiality, Symbolism, and Computational Formalization

Abstract

Antoni Gaudí (1852–1926) developed a chromatic system in which color was not decorative but structural, perceptual, symbolic, and temporal. His architecture integrates color as a function of material truth, natural reference, and light interaction. This document analyzes Gaudí’s dominant color palettes, provides quantified palette systems with hexadecimal color codes, and formalizes his chromatic logic for the purpose of developing an AI-based Color Expert Agent specialized in Gaudí’s work.

1. Gaudí’s Philosophy of Color

Gaudí understood color as a living phenomenon, inseparable from nature, material origin, light behavior, and emotional or spiritual perception. Color in his work emerges from material and reacts dynamically to light rather than remaining static or ornamental.

2. General Chromatic Principles

  • Nature-derived palettes
  • Material-first color selection
  • Fragmentation instead of flat color
  • Light-reactive surfaces
  • Symbolic but non-literal meanings
  • Controlled irregularity
  • Emotional and spiritual intent

4. Trencadís as a Chromatic System

Trencadís functions as chromatic engineering: breaking color into fragments to enhance reflectivity, avoid flatness, and generate perceptual movement. Computationally, it can be modeled through stochastic sampling and high-frequency color variation.

5. Color and Light as a Time-Based System

Gaudí designed color to evolve over time. Morning light emphasizes cool hues, while afternoon light intensifies warm tones. Interior stained glass transforms light itself into a chromatic emitter.
