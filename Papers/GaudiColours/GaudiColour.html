CORE COLOUR PHILOSOPHY SYMBOLIC COLOUR USAGE Gaudi assigns symbolic meaning to colour, particularly in religious contexts. Examples: - Blue: heaven, spirituality - Green: life, renewal - Red: sacrifice, vitality - Gold: divinity, light Rule: Colour choices often reinforce narrative or symbolism, not just aesthetics. --- LIGHT-DEPENDENT COLOUR Description: Gaudi designs colour to interact with natural light. Stained glass and ceramics change appearance over time. Characteristics: - Morning and evening colours differ - Shadows intensify saturation - Direct light reveals texture Rule: Colour should be evaluated in changing light conditions, not fixed illumination. --- COLOUR DISTRIBUTION RULES - Colour density varies across surfaces - No single dominant hue everywhere - Bright colours appear in focal zones - Neutral tones dominate structural masses Avoid: - Uniform colour fills - Minimalist palettes - High-contrast black and white schemes --- SUMMARY FOR MODEL CONDITIONING Gaudi-style colour is: - Material-driven - Fragmented and irregular - Inspired by nature - Reactive to light - Symbolically informed High-level instruction: When generating Gaudi-inspired visuals, use colour to enhance form, material, and light. Avoid flat, digital, or synthetic colour application.
